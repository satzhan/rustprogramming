"Vec expansion is done in powers of 2" — basically right, with a footnote.
Vec doubles when it grows (so you see 4 → 8 → 16 → 32 …), but only after the first allocation. It doesn't have to start at a power of 2 — Vec::with_capacity(7) gives you exactly 7 slots. The doubling kicks in when a push overflows capacity. The reason is amortized O(1): doubling means each element gets copied at most ~once on average across many pushes, no matter how many you do.
"VecDeque [grows] in powers of 2 too" — also basically right, with a stricter footnote.
VecDeque is more committed to powers of two than Vec is. Its capacity is essentially always rounded up to a power of two. The reason is the wraparound trick: computing buffer[(head + i) % capacity] is dirt cheap when capacity is a power of two, because % capacity collapses into a single bitmask operation (& (capacity - 1)). If capacity were 7, you'd need a real modulo — slower. So VecDeque accepts a little wasted space to keep that index math fast.
"Deque has 2 pointers of head and tail" — this is the half I want to refine.
It has two indices, not two pointers. Pointers point into memory; indices are just integers (usize) that label a slot in the buffer. The distinction matters because:

The buffer itself has one base pointer (the start of the heap allocation).
head and tail (or head + len, depending on the implementation) are integers that tell you where in the ring the logical front and back currently sit.
When the ring grows, the base pointer changes (new allocation), but head and tail don't need updating in the same way — they get rebased so that the data starts cleanly at index 0 again.

This is why LinkedList is the structure that actually has two pointers per element (a prev and a next on every node, plus head/tail pointers on the list header). VecDeque's "two-endedness" is bookkeeping on a flat array, not extra plumbing on each item.

A Vec is a one-way escalator — you can only board at the bottom and exit at the top, so when it fills, you build a taller escalator and re-place everyone in the same order.
A VecDeque is a carousel with a moving "front-of-line" sign. People board and leave from either end; the carousel itself doesn't move, but the sign does. When all the seats fill up, the operator builds a bigger carousel and seats everyone starting from seat 0 — but the carousel is still one continuous ring, not a chain of separate cars linked together.
A LinkedList, by contrast, is a freight train: each car is a separate chunk of metal welded to the next by physical couplings. Lighter to add a car at the end (no rebuilding), but every car carries the weight of two coupling pieces, and walking the train means stepping car-to-car-to-car.
The correction worth carrying forward
When you teach this or explain it on the audio, the cleanest one-liner is:

"VecDeque doesn't have two pointers — it has two indices into one allocation. The 'doubly' in 'double-ended' is about which ends you can touch, not about how the structure is built underneath."

That distinction is what trips students up the most, because the textbook pairing of "queue = singly linked, deque = doubly linked" plants the wrong picture.
